This guide walks you through every section of the form. You do not need technical expertise — only observe and record what you physically see at the site. When in doubt, always mark Unknown.
General Rules
✔

Only confirm what you see

Do not guess or assume. If a panel is locked and you cannot look inside, mark fields as Unknown rather than guessing.

✔

Checkboxes vs Radio buttons

Checkboxes ☐ — you can select more than one. Used for machine types, ports, brands, etc.
Radio buttons ◯ — pick only one. Used for Yes / No / Unknown questions.

✔

Take photos whenever possible

Photos save the technical team significant time. Even a quick phone photo of a nameplate, panel, or screen is highly valuable.

✔

Date format

Write the survey date as DD / MM / YYYY — for example: 03 / 02 / 2026

Section 1 — Customer & Site Information
1

Basic site details

Fill in details about who you are visiting and where. Ask the on-site contact for the exact plant unit name if it is not displayed on signage. Record their direct phone or email — the team may need to follow up.

Tip: The customer contact name and role helps us route questions to the right person later. If there are multiple contacts, write the most relevant one.
Example
Customer: Tata Steel Ltd    Plant: Jamshedpur Unit 3
Contact: Rajesh Mehta — Maintenance Manager
Phone: 0657-XXX-XXXX   Email: r.mehta@tatasteel.com
Section 2 — Machine Overview
2

Read the machine nameplate

Look for a nameplate — usually a metal plate bolted or welded to the machine body. It typically shows the manufacturer, model number, and year. If the nameplate is missing or unreadable, fill what you can and take a photo of the area.

For Machine Type, tick every option that applies. A furnace can also be a utility system, for example. Use "Other" only if none of the listed options fit.

Example
OEM: ABB    Model: IRB 6700    Year: 2020
Primary Function: Robotic welding arm for auto body panels
Machine Type: ☑ CNC / Production Machine

Section 3 — Hardware & Control System
3.1

Control Panel

A dedicated electrical panel is a separate box or cabinet that houses only this machine's wiring and controllers — usually grey or steel-coloured, sometimes with a tag number. If the panel door is locked, mark Panel Accessible: No and do not force it open.

A PLC (Programmable Logic Controller) is the brain of the machine. It looks like a small rectangular module mounted on a DIN rail inside the panel. The brand name is printed on the front face.

Tip: If you can see a PLC but cannot read the brand or model, take a close-up photo. The label is usually on the top or front of the module.

Spare I/O means unused input/output slots on the PLC — small empty gaps next to the wired connectors. Only tick "Yes" if you can clearly see empty slots.

3.2

Visible Ports

Look at the back, side, or top of the control panel or machine for any connectors or cables. Here is what common ports look like:

Ethernet (RJ45)Looks like a wide USB plug — 8 pins inside a rectangular clip.
M12Round industrial connector, common on machines exposed to dust or moisture.
RS-485 / RS-422 / RS-232Small 9-pin or 5-pin serial connectors — older style, sometimes labelled "COM".
USB (Service)Standard USB-A port. Often behind a small flap labelled "Service" or "Maintenance".
Type A / Type CUSB connector types - Type A is the standard rectangular USB, Type C is the newer reversible connector.

If a cable is already plugged into a port, mark Ports Already in Use: Yes and write what it connects to.

Section 4 — HMI & Computer
4

What is an HMI?

An HMI (Human Machine Interface) is the touchscreen or display panel operators use to monitor the machine. It shows real-time data but typically does NOT store historical data.

An Industrial PC is different - it's an actual computer that can store and process data. It usually runs Windows or Linux and has storage capabilities.

Tip: HMIs display data, computers store it. Fill both sections if the machine has both.
Section 5 — Data Availability
5

Where does data live?

This is about where the machine's readings actually get stored — not just displayed. Tick more than one box if applicable. A machine might show temperature on screen but also log it inside the PLC.

Note that HMI and Local PC are now separate options since only computers typically store data long-term.

For 5.2 Data Types Visible, list every kind of reading you can see anywhere at the machine.

Example — Data Types
Temperature (°C), furnace pressure (bar), cycle count, alarm log, energy consumption (kWh), RPM

For 5.3 Data Frequency, this means how often new data points are recorded — not how often you look at the screen. Ask the operator if unsure.

Section 6 — Network & IT
6

Network basics

Factory Network means the machine or its panel is connected via a cable to other systems in the plant — typically through an Ethernet switch (a small box with multiple ports, often mounted on a wall or in a rack).

WiFi Available means there is a wireless network that could potentially be used for connectivity in the area.

Firewall Present indicates network security measures that may affect data transmission.

IT Approval — some factories require formal sign-off before connecting any new device to their network. Ask the plant manager or IT contact.

Tip: Look for an Ethernet cable running from the machine or panel to a wall or ceiling. If you see one, an Ethernet switch is likely nearby — follow the cable.

Section 7 — Data Readiness Level
7

Pick the highest level that applies

This is the most important single answer on the form. Use the table below to decide.

0No digital data at all. Everything is written on paper or read by eye. No screen or controller shows numbers.
1Numbers appear on screen only. You can see live readings, but they are not saved anywhere. Once the display updates, old data is gone.
2Data is stored inside the machine. The PLC or HMI saves readings locally. You might export via USB, but nothing goes to a network.
3Data is on the factory network. It flows to a SCADA system, local server, or shared database that other computers can access.
4Data reaches the cloud. Already being sent to an off-site or cloud platform (e.g. AWS, Azure, or an IIoT dashboard).
Tip: If you are unsure, pick the level you are most confident about and explain in the Reason box.
Section 8 — Likely Integration Method
8

How will we connect to this machine?

This is a first guess — the technical team will confirm later. Use these guidelines:

Direct Connection/HMIThe machine has a PLC or accessible port. Data already exists digitally and can be tapped into.
AI Camera ReadingNo ports or PLC accessible. We place a camera in front of the display and use AI to read the numbers from the screen.
HybridSome data can come from a direct connection and some requires a camera. Or the situation needs more investigation.
Not SureTick this if you genuinely cannot tell. Write any observations in the Why box.
Section 9 — Constraints / Risks
9

What could get in the way?

Tick every constraint you come across. These are not failures — the technical team just needs to plan around them.

OEM RestrictionsThe machine manufacturer does not allow third-party modifications or connections.
Locked PanelsThe electrical panel is sealed. Only the OEM or a certified technician can open it.
No Network AccessThe machine is physically isolated — no cable or Wi-Fi reaches it.
Harsh EnvironmentThe area is very hot, dusty, or damp. Placing sensors or cameras needs special equipment.
Safety LimitsThe area has safety zones or restricted access rules during operation.
Tip: Be specific in the Details box. Example: "Panel locked — key held by Mr. Raju, Maintenance" or "Area exceeds 80°C during operation".
Section 10 — Photo Checklist
10

What to photograph and how

Photos are the single most useful thing you can bring back. Tick each box after you have taken that shot.

Machine NameplateClose-up of the metal plate — must be readable. Show model, serial number, year.
Panel InteriorInside the control panel with wiring and modules visible. Only if safely accessible.
Communication PortsAny ports or connectors visible on the panel or machine body. Include cables if plugged in.
HMI ScreenThe operator screen while showing live data. Capture software name if visible on the title bar.
Network CabinetAny switch box or network rack in or near the machine area.
Overall MachineA wide shot of the full machine in its surroundings — helps the team understand the physical layout.
Section 11 — Additional Comments
11

Anything else you noticed

Write anything that did not fit elsewhere. This often turns out to be the most useful part of the form. Common things to note:

Examples of useful comments
"Machine was not running during the visit — readings may differ when live"
"Operator mentioned a firmware upgrade is planned next month"
"There is a second identical machine on the same line — Line 4B"
"The plant manager wants a demo before approving network access"
